Project Overview

Verdura is an innovative Virtual Reality (VR) learning platform designed to bridge the gap between humans and the plant world. It aims to inspire a deeper connection with plant science through an immersive and engaging user experience.

Solution

Verdura leverages VR technology to create an immersive learning environment. It offers a variety of features to allow users to explore the world of plants:

  • Plant Evolution: Delve into the specifics of plant development, from seed germination to flowering and beyond.

  • Virtual Environment: Embark on a visually captivating VR journey through various ecosystems, enhancing your understanding of botany in a unique way.

  • Interactive Quizzes:Test your knowledge and solidify your learning by engaging in informative quizzes.

These features, along with a user-friendly interface that includes search, favorites, and progress tracking functionalities, aim to transform plant science education into a captivating adventure. Additionally, Verdura has the potential to be translated into multiple languages, broadening its reach to a global audience.

Problem

Traditional educational systems often have a blind spot when it comes to plant sciences. This results in a significant gap in our collective knowledge and appreciation for the plant world. Plants are the silent powerhouses of our planet, playing a critical role in maintaining the delicate balance of Earth's ecosystem. From producing the oxygen we breathe to providing the base of the food chain, plants are the foundation of life on Earth. Their intricate beauty goes beyond aesthetics, with unique adaptations and symbiotic relationships that sustain entire ecosystems. Unfortunately, current educational approaches often fail to capture this wonder. Textbooks and lectures often present a dry and one-dimensional view of plant life, neglecting the fascinating stories behind their growth and survival. This lack of engagement affects people of all ages, from students who might lose interest in pursuing plant-related careers to adults who lack a deeper understanding of the natural world around them. This knowledge gap hinders our ability to appreciate the interconnectedness of life and poses a challenge for future generations who will need to be responsible stewards of the environment.

Research Process

To understand user needs and preferences, Verdura employed a multi-pronged research approach:

Initial Hypothesis and Strategy

Our initial hypothesis was that university students would be the primary target audience for a botany learning app. Following this assumption, the initial strategy involved:

  • Conducting online surveys to gather user preferences regarding features and functionalities.

  • Scheduling individual interviews with Botany students to understand their specific needs and challenges in learning plant science.

Sampling

  • Snowball sampling: Recruited participants through existing networks to reach a wider audience.

  • Quota-based opportunity sampling: Ensured a mix of demographics within the target group (aged 13-33) to capture diverse perspectives.

Data Collection

  • Online surveys: Gathered general user preferences and interest levels.

  • Individual interviews with Botany BA students: Gained in-depth insights from experienced learners to understand specific needs and pain points.

Revised Approach

Based on the research findings, the target audience was broadened beyond university students to a more general audience aged 13-33. The focus shifted from age to user needs and interests:

  • Virtual Tour:Explore diverse ecosystems in stunning VR, fostering a sense of wonder and appreciation for the plant world.
  • Interactive Quizzes: Test and solidify botanical knowledge in a fun and engaging way.
  • Accessibility: The platform should cater to a wider range of interests and age groups, with an intuitive interface for ease of use.

Considering the VR headset options and the revised target audience, Oculus Quest 2 emerged as the most suitable choice due to its:

  • Stand-alone functionality:Doesn't require a PC or console, making it more accessible to a broader demographic.
  • Extensive content library: Offers a variety of VR experiences that can be integrated with Verdura's educational content.
  • User-friendliness: Easy to set up and use, catering to users with varying levels of technological expertise.
  • Ongoing updates and improvements:Oculus Quest 2 is actively supported by Meta, ensuring a smooth and up-to-date VR experience.

Findings and Analysis

Interview Results:

  • Students desired a virtual environment to explore diverse biomes, overcoming geographical limitations and fostering a deeper connection with plant life.
  • Testing knowledge was important, but plant identification was less interesting as a standalone feature.

Survey Results: (Participants aged 13-33):

  • Not everyone owned plants, making a VR gardening experience less universally appealing.
  • Users highly valued features like virtual tours and interactive quizzes for knowledge testing and engagement.
  • Interestingly, a higher percentage of female participants favored the name "Verdura" (meaning "green" in Italian).

VR Headset Selection

Verdura prioritized selecting a VR headset that could deliver an exceptional user experience while considering factors like accessibility, target audience, and content compatibility. Here's a breakdown of the VR headsets explored during the research phase:

  • Oculus Quest 2:This standalone headset offers portability, a large and growing library of games and applications, and a user-friendly interface, making it a strong contender for Verdura.
  • Valve Index: Known for its high-fidelity visuals and precise tracking, the Valve Index caters to a more enthusiast audience but might not be ideal for Verdura's broader target demographic due to its higher price point.
  • PlayStation VR (PSVR): While cost-effective and leveraging the PlayStation user base, PSVR requires a PlayStation console, limiting accessibility.
  • HTC Vive Cosmos Elite: Offering a good balance of features and ease of use, the Vive Cosmos Elite was another potential candidate.
  • HP Reverb G2: This high-resolution headset prioritizes visual quality but might not be the most cost-effective option for Verdura.

From all thesd considerations Oculus Quest 2 was selcted to be best performing VR tool for Verdura

Personas & Scenarios

Personas & Scenarios were developed to understand the diverse needs of potential users. These personas included Julia, a Teacher and Dave, a Botany Student. These personas helped shape the design process by providing specific insights into the challenges faced by users.

Low-Fidelity

To get an engaging VR experience allows you to explore the wonders of the coral reef at your own pace, fostering a deeper understanding and appreciation for this vital ecosystem, the low-fidelity phase involved creating a paper prototype for the application. This prototype outlined the basic structure, user interface, and key features. The initial design focused on touch to learn, zoom in, zoom out and interactive bubbles. This phase allowed for quick iterations and adjustments based on feedback.

Hi-Fidelity

The high-fidelity prototype evolved from the low-fidelity version, incorporating detailed design elements, color schemes, and user interface refinements. The design adhered to inclusive principles, ensuring that each feature was carefully implemented to cater to user needs.

User Testing

A semi-structured interview process with 8 participants (aged 14-45) explored user experience with a VR botany prototype. Participants were a mix of genders (3 male, 5 female) and all had prior VR experience.


Methodology:

    Moderated and exploratory approach with a mix of closed and open-ended questions.

  • Participants were briefed on the prototype content before playing it.

  • An initial round of questioning was followed by a replay with video pauses at specific points to capture user feedback on visuals and understanding.

Key Findings:

    Visual Clarity: All participants agreed that the prototype's visual clarity needed improvement, making it the top priority for future iterations.

  • Speed and Noticeability: The information presentation speed was too fast, and the green bubbles highlighting information points were not easily noticeable due to size and color.

  • Immersion: Opinions on immersion were divided (50% positive, 50% negative). Interestingly, all online participants felt immersed, while none of the in-person participants did. This suggests potential influences of factors like the "bystander effect" and comfort levels in different environments.

Additional Feedback:

    Text size and highlighting of difficult words were suggested for improved readability.

  • Users recommended including audio or text narration for guidance.

  • VR interface improvements were noted, such as a non-floating side menu and missing exit controls in pop-up boxes.


Overall, the user testing yielded valuable insights to refine the VR botany prototype. Addressing visual clarity, information presentation speed, and user interface elements will be crucial in creating a more engaging and effective learning experience.
